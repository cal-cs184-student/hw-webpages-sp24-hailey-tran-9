Overview

In this project, we created a cloth mesh that is supported by masses and springs to simulate its properties of physics over time (falling, bending, creasing). It also allowed us to incorporate parameters like density, ks, and damping to manipulate the shape of the cloth. We also implemented collisions with other objects and itself. This prevents the cloth from clipping. Finally, we dipped our toes into shaders, implementing basic diffuse, blinn-phong, texture mapping, bump & displacement mapping, and environment-mapped reflections, using the GLSL language.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Spring constant ks

Very low ks: The cloth falls and folds very slowly. There are also a lot of wrinkles in the cloth. The flaps of the cloth move continuously like it’s being blown on by a soft wind.

High ks: There’s very little folds and bending in the cloth. It also seems like the cloth withstands external forces better (gravity doesn’t weigh it down as much).

Density

Low density: The edge between the two pinned points of the cloth don’t concave as much; it seems like the cloth isn’t heavily weighed down. This makes sense because the density is lower, so the cloth isn’t being pulled too strongly towards the ground.

High density: The edge between the two pinned points of the cloth concaves in deeper. In contrast to low density, the cloth appears to be heavier, so the pinned points don’t hold up the non-pinned point masses as well.

Damping

Low damping: With very low damping, the cloth swings from side-to-side wildly. There’s also a lot of ripples that appear in the cloth.

High damping: With very high damping, the cloth falls slowly and very smoothly. It doesn’t swing at all and stops below the pinned points.

Observation: The ks value has a direct relationship with the stiffness of the cloth. The lower ks value (500) covered the sphere and conformed to its round shape, leaving many pleats in the cloth. The middle value (5000) is still rounded at the middle-top of the cloth, but it doesn’t conform to the sphere as well. This makes the smooth top look like a small bump. The largest ks value (50000) is very stiff and has less folds. Most of the rounded shape is also gone.

Cloth Falling

Varying Parameters

Decreased density (5) creates smoother waves on the cloth as it falls. Its fallen state is also more spread out and has less defined creases.

Increased density (500) creates more folds and wrinkles in the cloth. It also falls more densely packed together.

Decreased ks (5) creates more folds as the cloth falls (a similar effect to increasing the density). However, its fallen state is both more spread out and bumpy.

Increased ks (10000) is generally the same as the default value of 5000 as it falls. The cloth’s fallen state is more spread out with smooth, defined creases.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program is a isolated program that takes inputs (typically vertex positions, normals, color, etc…) and manipulates them for a certain output. It is used to change the appearance of an object by adjusting it’s lighting and color. Additionally, shader programs are run on the GPU as opposed to the CPU enhancing its performance. Here we used vertex and fragment files to implement the shaders. A vertex shader takes in some inputs (positions, normals, etc..), modifies, and outputs a final position. Vertex shaders are used as inputs to fragment shaders, which take these inputs calculated by the vertex shader to compute a final output color for the shading.

The Blinn-Phong model is a modification to the Phong model and makes up for its faults by introducing a different set of vectors for its computations such as the half-angle vector. The half-angle vector is the direction the surface normal needs to face in order to show a specular reflection. The blinn phong model accumulates light from the following components: ambient light, specular light, and diffuse light.
