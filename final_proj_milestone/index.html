Accomplishments & Preliminary Results

So far, we’ve been able to implement a basic fluid simulator with sphere particles representing the water’s body. The particles are able collide with spheres and not fall through the ground.

Collision between the particles and cubes is a work in progress. The initial collision is detected, but the particles sink into the cube as time progresses.

In order to accomplish this, we’ve done some research on the principles and mathematics of a Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) fluid simulation. The referenced paper “Particle-Based Fluid Simulation for Interactive Applications” by Matthias Müller, David Charypar and Markus Gross (linked under the Resources header) was incredibly helpful. The primary formulas that we derived from this paper, along with their descriptions and why we’re using them, are shown below.

Smoothing Kernels

Smoothing kernels are used in order to reduce the amount of “craziness” each particle has. The W(r,h) function (the smoothing kernel) returns a scalar value that determines the influence a particle has on its local neighbors.

Spiky Kernel

A spiky kernel is used for the computation of pressure forces. This is needed because pressured particles tend to clump up tightly, meaning little (but non-zero) repulsion should be applied. The general smoothing kernel doesn’t reflect this behavior.

Viscosity Kernel

A kernel that is used for the computation of viscosity forces. This kernel fixes the general smoothing kernel’s issue with negative values resulting from its Laplacian.

Density

m: Particle Mass

This is the equation to calculate the density of each particle. Note that this is mostly heavily reliant on its neighbors j and the distance between the two particles.

Reflection

Hurdles

Currently, our team is concerned about generalizing the collision system between the fluid simulation and irregular polygons. Our current solution checks the particles’ distance from singular objects (i.e. the particles against a sphere). This would be fine if the obstacles in our game were perfect shapes, but this isn’t ideal, as we’d like to design levels that feel and appear less artificial and clean-cut.

Breakthroughs

One of the biggest breakthroughs during the entire process was understanding how to implement the smoothing kernels for pressure and viscosity. Without them, we were confused as to why the particles were acting sporadically, and it was because particles that were too far to realistically affect other particles were influencing its behavior.
